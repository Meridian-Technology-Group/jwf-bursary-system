# 28 — What changes year-on-year

Backlink: [[README#Year two and beyond — re-assessment]]

When the Foundation re-assesses your bursary, they look at
**this year's** financial picture and compare it to **last year's**
to decide whether your award goes up, stays the same, or goes
down.

This guide is plain background — there's nothing you need to do
in the portal here. It's worth a read so you know how to read
the outcome letter when it arrives.

## The benchmark

The Foundation uses **last year's payable fees** (your share of
the school fees after last year's bursary) as the benchmark. If
your financial position has stayed roughly the same, your
contribution stays roughly the same — and the bursary covers any
increase in school fees.

## How decisions are explained

Re-assessment outcome letters reference **reason codes** for any
material change in your award. These are short, plain-English
codes such as:

- **Increase in income** — household income has risen
  significantly.
- **Reduction in income** — household income has fallen
  significantly.
- **New dependant** — a new child or elderly relative is now
  being supported.
- **Property sale / purchase** — your asset base has changed.
- **Inheritance received** — a one-off windfall has been
  reported.
- **Redundancy / job loss** — you've reported a loss of
  employment.

The letter lists every reason code that applied to your case and
the direction (up or down) it pushed your award.

## What this means for you

- **Provide explanations in [[14-section-9-additional-information]].**
  If there's a number in your income or assets that looks bigger
  or smaller than last year for a one-off reason (e.g. inherited a
  car you sold, took a sabbatical), say so. Assessors can apply a
  one-off reason code instead of a structural one.
- **Don't try to game the timing.** Foundation assessors look at
  income over the full tax year, not month-by-month.
- **Material changes go both ways.** If your income drops, your
  bursary may increase. Always declare the new figures honestly.

## If your award changes by a lot

A large change (up or down) usually means the assessor wants to
talk to you. Don't be alarmed — it's a conversation, not a
disciplinary process. They'll explain what they saw and what it
means for the new year.
